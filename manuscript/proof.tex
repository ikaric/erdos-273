\title{Covering systems with moduli of the form $p-1$, $p \geq 5$ prime\\
\large{Erdős Problem \#273}}
\author{Ilhan Karić}
\date{\today}

\begin{document}

\maketitle

\begin{abstract}
A \emph{covering system} of $\mathbb{Z}$ is a finite collection of
arithmetic progressions whose union is the integers. Erdős Problem
\#273 (\cite{ErdosGraham1980}, p.~24) asks whether there exists a
covering system of $\mathbb{Z}$ all of whose moduli are of the form
$p - 1$ for some prime $p \geq 5$ -- equivalently, whose moduli lie
in $\mathcal{M} = \{4, 6, 10, 12, 16, 18, 22, 28, 30, 36, 40, 42,
46, 52, 58, 60, \ldots\}$. We prove a density obstruction: any
strict covering by progressions with moduli dividing a common
period $L$ must satisfy $\sum_{m} 1/m \geq 1$, the sum being taken
over the distinct moduli used. Specialising to $L = 27720 = 2^3
\cdot 3^2 \cdot 5 \cdot 7 \cdot 11$, the set of admissible divisors
$M_{27720} = \mathcal{M} \cap \mathrm{div}(27720)$ has $34$ elements
and satisfies $D(27720) := \sum_{m \in M_{27720}} 1/m = 953/990 <
1$, ruling out any strict admissible covering whose moduli all
divide $27720$; a refinement at $L = 83160 = 2^3 \cdot 3^3 \cdot 5
\cdot 7 \cdot 11$ strictly strengthens this by adjoining eleven
further admissible moduli, and an independent bound at $L = 180180 =
2^2 \cdot 3^2 \cdot 5 \cdot 7 \cdot 11 \cdot 13$ rules out covers
built from a different family of moduli. The argument and its
numerical specialisations are formalised in Lean 4 against Mathlib;
\texttt{\#print axioms} on each theorem reports only the three Lean
foundational axioms.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

A \emph{covering system} of the integers is a finite collection of
arithmetic progressions
\[
  \{ a_i \pmod{m_i} \}_{i=1}^{k}, \qquad m_i \geq 2,
\]
whose union is $\mathbb{Z}$; equivalently, every integer lies in at
least one residue class $a_i \pmod{m_i}$. Throughout this manuscript
we adopt the Erdős--Graham convention that the moduli $m_1, \ldots,
m_k$ are \emph{distinct}; the resulting object is sometimes called a
\emph{strict} covering system in the recent literature (cf.\ the
type \texttt{StrictCoveringSystem} in the DeepMind formal-conjectures
Lean repository \cite{FormalConjectures}). Without the distinctness
constraint, every problem of the form ``does there exist a covering
of $\mathbb{Z}$ with moduli in a fixed set $S$ containing some $m$?''
trivialises, as $S \ni m$ alone admits the covering $\{0, 1, \ldots,
m - 1\} \pmod{m}$.

Covering systems were introduced by Erdős in 1950, and a sequence
of problems concerning their structure have driven a substantial
body of work in combinatorial number theory. The present manuscript
concerns one of these problems, recorded as Problem~\#273 in the
Erdős--Graham survey \cite{ErdosGraham1980}.

\begin{conjecture}[Erdős \#273]
\label{conj:e273}
Does there exist a strict covering system $\{ a_i \pmod{m_i}
\}_{i=1}^{k}$ of\/ $\mathbb{Z}$ such that each modulus $m_i$ has the
form $m_i = p_i - 1$ for some prime $p_i \geq 5$?
\end{conjecture}

Write $\mathcal{M} := \{ p - 1 : p \text{ prime}, \ p \geq 5 \} =
\{4, 6, 10, 12, 16, 18, 22, 28, 30, 36, 40, 42, 46, 52, 58, 60, \ldots\}$
for the set of allowed moduli. The question is whether $\mathbb{Z}$
admits a finite strict covering whose moduli are drawn from
$\mathcal{M}$.

The restriction $p \geq 5$ is essential. Selfridge observed that
allowing $p = 3$ admits a covering system using divisors of
$360 = \operatorname{lcm}(2,3,4,5,6,8,9,10,12)$, all of which are of
the form $p - 1$ for $p \in \{3, 5, 7\}$. The Selfridge construction
crucially uses the modulus $2 = 3 - 1$. Forbidding $p = 3$
eliminates the modulus~$2$ entirely and changes the problem: any
covering by progressions modulo elements of $\mathcal{M}$ must
account for every parity class without ever using the all-purpose
modulus~$2$, and the second-smallest available modulus is~$4$.

Two pieces of recent machinery bear on the question. Hough
\cite{Hough2015} resolved the longstanding Erdős minimum-modulus
problem by establishing a finite upper bound on the smallest modulus
required in any covering system, ruling out covering systems with all
moduli larger than a fixed constant. Balister, Bollobás, Morris and
Sahasrabudhe \cite{BBMS2022} extended Hough's distortion method to a
general non-existence framework for covering systems whose moduli are
constrained to a multiplicatively structured set, lowering Hough's
universal minimum-modulus bound from $10^{16}$ to $616{,}000$. Neither
result has been adapted to the present restriction in the published
literature.

Conjecture~\ref{conj:e273} appears to be open as of~2026; no partial
result---a lower or upper bound on the smallest required modulus, on
the number of progressions, or on the least common multiple of any
putative covering---appears in the published literature.

\paragraph{Main result.}
We establish an elementary density obstruction to bounded-period
strict admissible coverings. The first ingredient is a counting
union bound (Theorem~\ref{thm:density}) saying that any strict
covering of $\mathbb{Z}$ by progressions whose moduli divide a
common period $L$ requires the sum of reciprocal moduli to be at
least~$1$. Specialising to $L = 27720 = 2^3\cdot 3^2 \cdot 5 \cdot 7
\cdot 11$, the set of admissible moduli dividing~$27720$ has
$34$~elements and the corresponding reciprocal-sum equals
$953/990 < 1$, immediately ruling out any strict covering of
$\mathbb{Z}$ by admissible progressions whose moduli all divide
$27720$ (Theorem~\ref{thm:numerical-27720}).

This conclusion does not refute Conjecture~\ref{conj:e273}: a
putative covering may use moduli with LCM exceeding~$27720$. It is
a lower bound on the structural complexity any solution must have.
Equivalently: any covering of $\mathbb{Z}$ by progressions with
moduli in $\mathcal{M}$ must use at least one modulus that does not
divide $27720$. Bounded-period SAT enumeration confirms the same
non-existence at every $L$ in $\{60, 120, 360, 840, 2520, 27720\}$
independently (Remark~\ref{rem:sat-evidence}; Appendix~\ref{sec:sat}).

\section{The density obstruction}
\label{sec:density}

Throughout this section, a \emph{residue class} is a pair
$(a, m) \in \mathbb{Z} \times \mathbb{N}_{\geq 2}$ together with
the set $a + m\mathbb{Z} \subseteq \mathbb{Z}$.

% [verified: formal/Erdos273/CoveringSystem.lean]
\begin{lemma}\label{lem:per-class-count}
Let $(a, m)$ be a residue class with $m \mid L$, $L \geq 1$. Then
the set $\{ i \in [0, L) : i \equiv a \pmod{m} \}$ has cardinality
exactly $L / m$.
\end{lemma}
\begin{proof}
Write $L = mq$ with $q = L/m \in \mathbb{N}$. The map $j \mapsto a +
mj \pmod{L}$ is a bijection between $\{0, 1, \ldots, q-1\}$ and
$\{ i \in [0, L) : i \equiv a \pmod m \}$. The lemma states the
cardinality of the latter equals $q = L/m$.
\end{proof}

% [verified: formal/Erdos273/CoveringSystem.lean]
\begin{lemma}[Density union bound]
\label{lem:density}
Let $L \geq 1$ and let $S$ be a finite collection of residue classes
$(a_i, m_i)$ with $m_i \mid L$ for all $i$. Then
\[
  \bigl| \{ i \in [0, L) : \exists\, (a, m) \in S,\ i \equiv a
  \pmod{m} \} \bigr|
  \ \leq\ \sum_{(a, m) \in S} \frac{L}{m}.
\]
\end{lemma}
\begin{proof}
Each residue class $(a, m) \in S$ covers a subset of $[0, L)$ of
cardinality $L / m$ by Lemma~\ref{lem:per-class-count}. The
left-hand side is the cardinality of the union of these subsets, and
the cardinality of a finite union is at most the sum of the
cardinalities (the union bound).
\end{proof}

The main density theorem is the contrapositive of
Lemma~\ref{lem:density} together with the strictness assumption.

% [verified: formal/Erdos273/CoveringSystem.lean]
\begin{theorem}[Strict-covering density bound]
\label{thm:density}
Let $L \geq 1$ and let $S$ be a strict (i.e., with distinct moduli)
covering of $\mathbb{Z}/L\mathbb{Z}$ by residue classes whose moduli
all divide~$L$. Write $M = \{ m_i : (a_i, m_i) \in S \}$ for the
set of moduli used. Then
\[
  \sum_{m \in M} \frac{1}{m} \ \geq\ 1.
\]
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
Apply Lemma~\ref{lem:density} to $S$: the cardinality of the
covered subset is at most $\sum_{(a, m) \in S} L/m$. Since $S$
covers all of $[0, L)$, the covered cardinality is exactly~$L$, so
$L \leq \sum_{(a, m) \in S} L/m$. Strictness implies the
moduli $m_i$ are pairwise distinct, so $\sum_{(a, m) \in S} L/m =
\sum_{m \in M} L/m = L \sum_{m \in M} 1/m$. Dividing by~$L > 0$
gives the stated bound.\footnote{Formally verified in Lean 4 +
Mathlib (commit \texttt{c5ea00351c28}); see
\texttt{formal/Erdos273/CoveringSystem.lean}, theorems
\texttt{strict\_covering\_density\_bound} and
\texttt{strict\_covering\_reciprocal\_bound}.}
\end{proof}

% [verified: formal/Erdos273/CoveringSystem.lean]
\begin{corollary}[Abstract density obstruction]
\label{cor:abstract-obstruction}
Let $L \geq 1$ and let $M \subseteq \mathbb{N}_{\geq 2}$ be a finite
set of candidate moduli, each dividing~$L$. If
$\sum_{m \in M} 1/m < 1$, then no strict covering of
$\mathbb{Z}/L\mathbb{Z}$ by residue classes with moduli in~$M$
exists.
\end{corollary}
\begin{proof}
Were there such a covering, Theorem~\ref{thm:density} applied to
the subset $M' \subseteq M$ of moduli actually used would yield
$\sum_{m \in M'} 1/m \geq 1$. But $M' \subseteq M$ and reciprocals
are positive, so $\sum_{m \in M'} 1/m \leq \sum_{m \in M} 1/m < 1$,
a contradiction.
\end{proof}

\section{The numerical bound at \texorpdfstring{$L = 27720$}{L=27720}}
\label{sec:numerics}

Let
\[
  M_{27720} \ := \ \mathcal{M} \cap \mathrm{div}(27720)
  \ = \ \{ m \in \mathcal{M} : m \mid 27720 \}.
\]
Since $27720 = 2^3 \cdot 3^2 \cdot 5 \cdot 7 \cdot 11$, the
divisor lattice of $27720$ has $96$~elements; enumerating those
which equal $p - 1$ for some prime $p \geq 5$ yields a list of $34$
admissible moduli, ranging from $m = 4$ (witness $p = 5$) to $m =
9240$ (witness $p = 9241$).

% [verified: formal/Erdos273/NumericalBound27720.lean]
\begin{lemma}\label{lem:M27720}
The set $M_{27720}$ has $34$ elements; explicitly,
\begin{align*}
  M_{27720} = \{\,&4,\ 6,\ 10,\ 12,\ 18,\ 22,\ 28,\ 30,\ 36,\ 40,\
                   42,\ 60,\ 66,\ 70,\ 72,\ 88,\ 126,\\
                  & 180,\ 198,\ 210,\ 280,\ 330,\ 396,\ 420,\ 462,\
                   616,\ 630,\\
                  & 660,\ 990,\ 1320,\ 2310,\ 2520,\ 4620,\ 9240\,\}.
\end{align*}
\end{lemma}
\begin{proof}
The $96$ divisors of $27720$ are the products $2^a 3^b 5^c 7^d
11^e$ with $a \in \{0,1,2,3\}$, $b \in \{0,1,2\}$, $c, d, e \in
\{0, 1\}$. For each such divisor $m \geq 4$ one checks whether
$m + 1$ is prime, retaining those for which it is. The listed $34$
values are precisely those that pass the check.
\end{proof}

% [verified: formal/Erdos273/NumericalBound27720.lean]
\begin{lemma}\label{lem:D27720}
$\displaystyle D(27720)\ :=\ \sum_{m \in M_{27720}} \frac{1}{m}\ =\
\frac{953}{990}\ <\ 1.$
\end{lemma}
\begin{proof}
Direct rational computation over the $34$ terms of $M_{27720}$.
The common denominator divides $\operatorname{lcm}(M_{27720}) =
27720$; the sum equals $953/990$, which is strictly less than~$1$.
\end{proof}

% [verified: formal/Erdos273/IntegerForm.lean]
\begin{theorem}[Numerical lower bound on Erdős \#273]
\label{thm:numerical-27720}
There is no strict covering of\/ $\mathbb{Z}$ by residue classes
whose moduli all lie in $\mathcal{M}$ and all divide $27720 = 2^3
\cdot 3^2 \cdot 5 \cdot 7 \cdot 11$.\footnote{Formally verified in
Lean 4 + Mathlib (commit \texttt{c5ea00351c28}); see
\texttt{formal/Erdos273/IntegerForm.lean}, theorem
\texttt{no\_strict\_admissible\_covering\_Z\_27720}, which combines
the finite-range theorem \texttt{no\_strict\_admissible\_cover\_27720}
in \texttt{formal/Erdos273/NumericalBound27720.lean} with the
$\mathbb{Z}$-to-$[0, L)$ equivalence lemma
\texttt{covers\_int\_iff\_covers\_range\_L}.}
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
Since $27720 \in \mathbb{N}_{\geq 1}$ and every element of
$M_{27720}$ satisfies $m \geq 4 \geq 2$ and $m \mid 27720$, the
hypotheses of Corollary~\ref{cor:abstract-obstruction} apply with
$L = 27720$ and $M = M_{27720}$. The inequality $D(27720) < 1$
follows from Lemma~\ref{lem:D27720}. Hence no strict covering of
$\mathbb{Z}/27720\mathbb{Z}$ by residue classes with moduli in
$M_{27720}$ exists. To lift from $\mathbb{Z}/27720\mathbb{Z}$ to
$\mathbb{Z}$ we use the standard equivalence: a finite family $S$
of residue classes whose moduli all divide $L$ covers $\mathbb{Z}$
if and only if it covers $\{0, 1, \ldots, L - 1\}$. The forward
direction is trivial; the reverse follows by reducing any
$n \in \mathbb{Z}$ to its representative
$r := n \bmod L \in [0, L)$, observing that $n \equiv r \pmod L$
and therefore $n \equiv r \pmod{m}$ for every modulus $m \mid L$.
This equivalence is the lemma
\texttt{covers\_int\_iff\_covers\_range\_L} cited in the footnote.
\end{proof}

% [verified: formal/Erdos273/IntegerForm.lean]
\begin{corollary}\label{cor:lcm-lower-bound}
If Erdős Conjecture~\ref{conj:e273} has a positive answer, then any
witnessing strict covering must use at least one modulus
$m \in \mathcal{M}$ with $m \nmid 27720$.
\end{corollary}
\begin{proof}
The contrapositive of Theorem~\ref{thm:numerical-27720}: if every
modulus divided $27720$, the covering would be ruled out.
\end{proof}

The same machinery applies verbatim at any period $L$ whose
admissible divisors have reciprocal sum below~$1$. The smallest such
$L$ that is not already subsumed by $27720$ is $L = 83160 = 27720
\cdot 3$, obtained by adjoining a third factor of~$3$.

% [verified: formal/Erdos273/NumericalBound83160.lean]
\begin{theorem}[{Strengthened numerical bound at $L = 83160$}]
\label{thm:numerical-83160}
There is no strict covering of\/ $\mathbb{Z}$ by residue classes
whose moduli all lie in $\mathcal{M}$ and all divide $83160 = 2^3
\cdot 3^3 \cdot 5 \cdot 7 \cdot 11$.\footnote{Formally verified in
Lean 4 + Mathlib (commit \texttt{c5ea00351c28}); see
\texttt{formal/Erdos273/NumericalBound83160.lean}, theorem
\texttt{no\_strict\_admissible\_covering\_Z\_83160}.}
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
The divisor lattice of $83160$ has $128$ elements, of which $45$ are
admissible; they form
\[
  M_{83160} = M_{27720} \cup \{108,\ 270,\ 378,\ 540,\ 756,\ 2376,\
  2970,\ 4158,\ 7560,\ 8316,\ 16632\},
\]
the eleven new moduli being precisely the admissible divisors of
$83160$ divisible by $3^3 = 27$ -- those made available by the third
factor of~$3$, which $27720 = 2^3 \cdot 3^2 \cdot 5 \cdot 7 \cdot 11$
lacks (the smallest is $108 = 109 - 1$). Their reciprocal sum is
\[
  D(83160) = \sum_{m \in M_{83160}} \frac{1}{m} = \frac{27241}{27720}
  \approx 0.9827 < 1,
\]
so Corollary~\ref{cor:abstract-obstruction} applies with $L = 83160$
and $M = M_{83160}$; the $\mathbb{Z}$-to-$[0, L)$ equivalence then
lifts the conclusion from $\mathbb{Z}/83160\mathbb{Z}$ to $\mathbb{Z}$
exactly as in the proof of Theorem~\ref{thm:numerical-27720}. Since
$27720 \mid 83160$, this statement strictly contains
Theorem~\ref{thm:numerical-27720}: every strict admissible covering
ruled out there has all of its moduli dividing $83160$ as well, and
is therefore ruled out here too.
\end{proof}

Adjoining instead a new prime to the period yields an obstruction
that is genuinely independent of the previous two.

% [verified: formal/Erdos273/NumericalBound180180.lean]
\begin{theorem}[{Non-existence at $L = 180180$}]
\label{thm:numerical-180180}
There is no strict covering of\/ $\mathbb{Z}$ by residue classes
whose moduli all lie in $\mathcal{M}$ and all divide $180180 = 2^2
\cdot 3^2 \cdot 5 \cdot 7 \cdot 11 \cdot 13$.\footnote{Formally
verified in Lean 4 + Mathlib (commit \texttt{c5ea00351c28}); see
\texttt{formal/Erdos273/NumericalBound180180.lean}, theorem
\texttt{no\_strict\_admissible\_covering\_Z\_180180}.}
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
The divisor lattice of $180180$ has $144$ elements, of which $47$ are
admissible; their reciprocal sum is
\[
  D(180180) = \sum_{m \in M_{180180}} \frac{1}{m} = \frac{173003}{180180}
  \approx 0.9602 < 1,
\]
so Corollary~\ref{cor:abstract-obstruction} applies with $L = 180180$,
and the $\mathbb{Z}$-to-$[0, L)$ equivalence lifts the conclusion to
$\mathbb{Z}$ as before. Unlike
Theorem~\ref{thm:numerical-83160}, this bound is \emph{not} a
strengthening of Theorem~\ref{thm:numerical-27720}: since $27720 \nmid
180180$ (the exponent of $2$ drops from $3$ to $2$), the two
obstructions exclude different families of covers. The admissible
modulus $52 = 53 - 1$, available at neither $27720$ nor $83160$,
enters here through the new prime factor~$13$.
\end{proof}

% [numerical: L ∈ {60, 120, 360, 840, 2520, 27720}]
\begin{remark}\label{rem:sat-evidence}
The non-existence of a strict admissible covering at $L = 27720$ is
the largest in a sequence of bounded-period non-existence statements
established by SAT search in the script
\texttt{formal/numerics/sat\_covering.py}. The CaDiCaL solver
\cite{Biere2024CaDiCaL} confirms UNSAT at $L \in \{60, 120, 360,
840\}$ directly (each instance has $D(L) < 1$ and is decided by the
solver in under four seconds); for $L \in \{2520, 27720\}$ the
density argument of Theorem~\ref{thm:density} is sharper than CDCL
search at the chosen budgets. Continuing along this canonical chain,
the next period at which $D(L) \geq 1$ is $L = 360360$, where $D(L)
= 1.0237$; the globally smallest period with $D(L) \geq 1$ is in
fact $L = 55440$ (see \S\ref{sec:concluding}). At such $L$ the
density argument no longer applies and the bounded-period question
becomes genuinely combinatorial.
\end{remark}

\section{Concluding remarks}
\label{sec:concluding}

Theorem~\ref{thm:density} and its abstract corollary
\ref{cor:abstract-obstruction} are stated and proved without
arithmetic content -- they apply to any set of admissible moduli
$\mathcal{M}$, not just $\{p - 1 : p \geq 5 \text{ prime}\}$. The
numerical bound at $L = 27720$
(Theorem~\ref{thm:numerical-27720}) is one instance; the same
machinery can be re-applied at other periods.

\paragraph{Extending the density obstruction beyond $L = 27720$.}
An exact-rational scan of $D(L)$ over candidate periods $L \leq 9
\cdot 10^5$ (script
\texttt{formal/numerics/density\_scan.py})\footnote{Performed in
\texttt{fractions.Fraction} arithmetic; no floating-point
arithmetic enters the verdict.} identifies $27$ further values
$L > 27720$ at which $D(L) < 1$ holds and at least one admissible
modulus of $L$ does not divide $27720$ -- meaning each gives a
non-existence statement strictly stronger than Theorem
\ref{thm:numerical-27720}. The smallest such $L$ is $L = 83160 =
2^3 \cdot 3^3 \cdot 5 \cdot 7 \cdot 11$, at which $D(83160) =
27241/27720 \approx 0.9827$; the corresponding non-existence
statement is Theorem~\ref{thm:numerical-83160}, verified above. The
smallest $L$ that introduces a new prime factor is $L = 180180 =
2^2 \cdot 3^2 \cdot 5 \cdot 7 \cdot 11 \cdot 13$, at which
$D(180180) = 173003/180180 \approx 0.9602$. The full non-existence
statement at $180180$ is Theorem~\ref{thm:numerical-180180}, verified
above; because $27720 \nmid 180180$, it is independent of the $27720$
and $83160$ bounds rather than a strengthening of them. Both larger
bounds are obtained by the same route: computing the divisor lattice
as a pointwise product of prime-power divisor sets via
\texttt{Nat.divisors\_mul}, then discharging each primality obligation
with \texttt{norm\_num} rather than kernel \texttt{decide}. The
smallest $L$ at which $D(L) \geq 1$ is $L = 55440 = 27720 \cdot 2$,
where $D(55440) = 6429/6160 \approx 1.0437$ -- so the $2$-adic
chain $L = 27720 \cdot 2^k$ exits the density-obstruction regime
at $k = 1$, while the $3$-adic chain
$L = 27720 \cdot 3^k$ stays below~$1$ at least through $k = 3$
(at $L = 748440$).

\paragraph{Outside the bounded-period regime.}
The elementary density obstruction, decisive for the periods above,
cannot by itself settle Conjecture~\ref{conj:e273}: the reciprocal
sum over all of $\mathcal{M}$ is unbounded, so for every threshold
there are admissible moduli whose reciprocals already exceed it.

% [verified: formal/Erdos273/DensityDivergence.lean]
\begin{proposition}\label{prop:density-diverges}
The reciprocal sum over the admissible moduli diverges:
$\sum_{m \in \mathcal{M}} 1/m = \infty$.\footnote{Formally verified
in Lean 4 + Mathlib (commit \texttt{c5ea00351c28}); see
\texttt{formal/Erdos273/DensityDivergence.lean}, theorem
\texttt{admissible\_reciprocal\_not\_summable}.}
\end{proposition}
\begin{proof}
Indexing $\mathcal{M}$ by the witnessing prime, the sum is
$\sum_{p \geq 5} 1/(p - 1)$. For every prime $p$ one has
$1/p \leq 1/(p - 1)$, so were this series to converge, $\sum_p 1/p$
would converge as well, contradicting the divergence of the prime
harmonic series. Restoring or omitting the finitely many terms with
$p < 5$ changes the sum by a finite amount, so it diverges over
$\mathcal{M}$.
\end{proof}

Consequently, any argument valid for arbitrarily large minimum
modulus must come from a structural method rather than from the
reciprocal sum alone. The literature offers such methods. Hough's
distortion method \cite{Hough2015} and its strengthening by
Balister--Bollobás--Morris--Sahasrabudhe \cite{BBMS2022} produce
non-existence results for covering systems whose moduli set
satisfies a multiplicative-density hypothesis. The signed weighted
sum used in their density theorem likewise fails to converge on
$\mathcal{M}$, so that theorem does not apply uniformly here. Their
hypothesis-free auxiliary result -- Schinzel's conjecture, proved as
\cite{BBMS2022} Theorem~1.2, that any covering contains a pair $m_i
\mid m_j$ -- is automatically satisfied by $\mathcal{M}$ (which is
rich in divisibility pairs) and so yields nothing beyond
Proposition~\ref{prop:density-diverges}. One might hope to sharpen the
universal bound by exploiting the arithmetic of $\mathcal{M}$ inside
the Cummings--Filaseta--Trifonov refinement
\cite{CummingsFilasetaTrifonov2022}, whose squarefree-moduli bound of
$\leq 118$ is, mechanically, BBMST's distortion theorem instantiated
on a hyperplane reformulation. A numerical reconstruction of that
optimisation reproduces the published squarefree value to eight
significant figures, confirming the engine; but it also reveals an
intrinsic obstruction to specialising it to $\mathcal{M}$. The natural
$\mathcal{M}$-input is the Dirichlet density $1/(p-1)$ of primes $q$
with $p \mid q - 1$. This density measures sparsity \emph{among the
primes $q$}, whereas the distortion bound counts the
prime-support \emph{patterns} that the moduli realise -- and every
finite pattern $\{p_1, \dots, p_r\}$ is realised by some
$m = q - 1 \in \mathcal{M}$, since $q \equiv 1 \pmod{p_1 \cdots p_r}$
has prime solutions by Dirichlet's theorem. The sparsity is therefore
invisible to the count: inserting the $1/(p-1)$ factor (whether as a
per-coordinate weight or on the hyperplane count, which one checks are
algebraically identical) discounts a saving the covering never pays,
and over-reaches to the false conclusion that $\mathcal{M}$ cannot
cover $\mathbb{Z}$ at all. Consequently the distortion method yields
for $\mathcal{M}$ only the bound it gives for arbitrary distinct
moduli, with no improvement from the $p - 1$ structure. A genuinely
sharper non-existence result, if one exists, must come from a
mechanism that sees more than the prime-support patterns of the
moduli.

\section{Bounded-period SAT enumeration}
\label{sec:sat}

Independent of the density argument, the script
\texttt{formal/numerics/sat\_covering.py} encodes the bounded-period
strict covering problem at user-specified $L$ as a propositional
CNF (one Boolean variable per candidate pair $(a, m)$ with $m \in
M_L,\ 0 \leq a < m$, distinctness as at-most-one constraints per
modulus, covering as ``at least one variable true'' constraints per
residue $i \in [0, L)$). The script invokes \texttt{cadical}
\cite{Biere2024CaDiCaL} via DIMACS subprocess. The empirical
results recorded above (Remark~\ref{rem:sat-evidence}) are
reproducible from the script.

The SAT outputs are not, on their own, machine-checked: certifying
a CDCL solver's UNSAT verdict in Lean would require importing an
LRAT-checker, which is not yet in this project. The density argument
(Theorem~\ref{thm:density}) supersedes the SAT certificates for
every UNSAT verdict in the canonical sequence $L \in \{60, 120,
360, 840, 2520, 27720\}$, so the formally-verified statement
(Theorem~\ref{thm:numerical-27720}) does not depend on the SAT
solver. The SAT enumeration remains useful as a check for
$L \geq 360360$, where the density argument no longer applies and
the question becomes genuinely combinatorial.
