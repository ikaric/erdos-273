**Subject:** Hough 2015 + BBMST 2022 + CFT 2022 + KKL 2022, with focus on whether the distortion method can be specialized to $\mathcal{M} = \{p-1 : p \text{ prime}, p \geq 5\}$ to yield a sharper minimum-modulus bound than the universal $10^{16}$.

## Summary

The minimum-modulus bound has dropped Hough $10^{16}$ (2015) → BBMST $615{,}999$ (2022) → CFT *with the squarefree restriction* $\leq 118$ (2022). Specialization to a restricted modulus set is *exactly* the kind of input the post-2022 distortion machinery accepts as a parameter, but it must be plugged into the right slot — specifically, into the geometric "hyperplane covering of $S_1 \times \cdots \times S_n$" reformulation (BBMST Theorem 2.1 of the survey) where $|S_j|$ is the multiplicity with which the $j$-th prime appears across moduli. CFT 2022's $\leq 118$ bound for squarefree distinct moduli is essentially the BBMST machine instantiated on $S_j = \{1, 2, \ldots, p_j\}$ (every prime contributes a single coordinate); the squarefreeness is what makes $|S_j| = p_j$ exactly, which in turn drives the Mertens-style estimate in their Lemma 4.7. **The corresponding "$\mathcal{M}$-instantiation" question is not whether the moduli are squarefree but how often the prime $p$ divides elements of $\mathcal{M}$ — i.e., the distribution of $\nu_p(q-1)$ as $q$ ranges over primes $\geq 5$.** This is a *concrete* analytic-NT question with established estimates (Erdős–Murty 1997 on prime divisors of $p-1$; Cohn–Lenstra heuristics) and is the lever to pull. The headline obstruction the literature already names: $\nu_2(p-1) \geq 1$ always (so every modulus is even), $\nu_2(p-1) = 1$ for primes $p \equiv 3\pmod 4$ (density $1/2$ in primes), $\nu_2(p-1) \geq 2$ for $p \equiv 1\pmod 4$, etc. — these give an exact distribution on the prime 2 coordinate that *should* yield a sharper distortion bound than universal moduli admit.

Conservative ROI estimate: a 1–2 session targeted analyst+formalist effort can produce a *sharper-than-$10^{16}$ minimum-modulus bound for $\mathcal{M}$-restricted covering systems* by plugging the explicit $S_j$-multiplicity sequence for $\mathcal{M}$ into BBMST Theorem 3.1. The bound will likely not be small (CFT-118 uses the cleaner squarefree structure), but a *quantitatively sharper* bound than $616{,}000$ is genuinely achievable and is novel ground.

## 1. Hough 2015 — the original distortion argument

**Reference.** Bob Hough, *Solution of the minimum modulus problem for covering systems*, Annals of Math. 181:1 (2015), 361–382. arXiv:1307.0874.

### 1.1 Theorem statement

> **Theorem 1 (Hough 2015).** The least modulus of a distinct covering system is at most $10^{16}$.

(The arXiv abstract says $10^{18}$; the published version drops this to $10^{16}$, per acknowledgements: "An anonymous referee pointed out a numerical improvement to the parameters which lowered the final bound." Both numbers appear in the literature — $10^{16}$ is the canonical one cited by BBMST and all downstream papers.)

### 1.2 Proof structure — five ingredients

Let $\mathcal{M} \subset \{m \in \mathbb{N} : m > M\}$ be a finite multiset of distinct moduli, with congruence classes $a_m \pmod m$ for each $m$. Hough wants to show the uncovered set $R = \bigcap_m (a_m \pmod m)^c$ has positive density.

**(i) Filtration by prime-factor size.** Pick an increasing sequence of thresholds $1 = P_{-1} < P_0 < P_1 < \cdots$ with $P_i \to \infty$. Let $Q_i = \prod_{p \leq P_i} p^{v_p(Q)}$ where $Q = \mathrm{lcm}(\mathcal{M})$. Then $\mathcal{M}_i = \{m \in \mathcal{M} : m \mid Q_i\}$ are the "$P_i$-smooth moduli." The sets $R_i = \bigcap_{m \in \mathcal{M}_i} (a_m \pmod m)^c$ shrink to $R$.

**(ii) Conditional measure on fibres.** $\mathbb{Z}/Q_{i+1}\mathbb{Z}$ is fibred over $\mathbb{Z}/Q_i\mathbb{Z}$. For each fibre $(r \bmod Q_i)$ Hough estimates the density of $R_{i+1}$ within it.

**(iii) The "new moduli" set.** $\mathcal{N}_{i+1} = \{n : n \mid Q_{i+1}, n > 1, p \mid n \Rightarrow p \in (P_i, P_{i+1}]\}$ — squarefree (or fully prime-power) numbers built from primes in the next layer. Each new modulus $m \in \mathcal{M}_{i+1} \setminus \mathcal{M}_i$ uniquely factors as $m = m_0 n$ with $m_0 \mid Q_i$ and $n \in \mathcal{N}_{i+1}$.

**(iv) Lovász Local Lemma.** Hough applies a *relative* form of the Local Lemma (the standard form gives Eq. (7); the stronger relative form gives Eq. (6)) to get $\lambda$-good fibres on which the sieve at stage $i+1$ is well-behaved. The weight function in the LLL is $x_n = e^{\lambda \omega(n)} |A_{n,r} \bmod n Q_i|/n$, where $\omega(n)$ is the number of distinct prime factors. The key parameter is $\lambda \geq 0$.

**(v) Bias statistics.** Hough introduces *moment statistics* $\beta_k^l(i) = \sum_{m \mid Q_i} \ell_k(m) \max_b \mu_i(R_{i-1}^* \cap R_i \cap (b \bmod m))/\mu_i(R_{i-1}^* \cap R_i)$, where $\ell_k(m) = (2^k-1)^{\omega(m)}$ for squarefree $m$ counts $k$-tuples with lcm $m$. Inductively, Proposition 3 controls how these bias statistics grow stage-to-stage:
$$\beta_k^l(i+1) \leq \frac{\beta_k^l(i)}{\pi_i^{\text{good}}} \prod_{P_i < p \leq P_{i+1}} \left(1 + e^\lambda \sum_{j=1}^{v_p} \frac{(j+1)^k - j^k}{p^j}\right).$$
At stage 0 the statistic is $\beta_k^l(0) \approx (\log P_0)^{2^k - 1}$.

### 1.3 Where the $10^{16}$ comes from

The bound $M \geq 10^{16}$ is the smallest minimum-modulus $M$ for which the explicit constraints (C0) ($\sum_{m > M, p|m \Rightarrow p \leq P_0} 1/m < \delta$ — initial-stage density) and (C1) (the inductive bias-control constraint) can be simultaneously satisfied for *some* choice of parameters $\delta, P_0, P_i, \lambda, \pi_i^{\text{good}}, k$. Hough's parameters were optimized numerically in Pari/GP; the proof requires explicit numerical estimation of primorial-like products $\prod_p (1 + e^\lambda/(p-1))$ via Rosser–Schoenfeld effective Mertens estimates. **The bound $10^{16}$ is essentially a numerical artifact: it is what falls out of the constraint system when one optimizes the free parameters with no extra structural information on the moduli set.**

### 1.4 Where the moduli set enters

The argument uses *no* structural property of $\mathcal{M}$ beyond:
- distinctness (Hough does not directly use this — it enters via Erdős's original definition that moduli are distinct integers > 1);
- $m > M$ (each modulus exceeds the minimum-modulus threshold $M$);
- $m \mid Q$ (each modulus divides the lcm, trivially).

The structural-property slot is the *prime-factorization profile* — how many moduli have a given factorization. In Hough's bias statistic $\beta_k^l(i)$, the sum is over **all** $m \mid Q_i$, weighted only by their lcm-multiplicities $\ell_k(m)$. **There is no place in Hough's argument where one can plug in "the moduli all have form $p-1$" except by restricting $\mathcal{M}_i \subseteq \mathcal{M} \cap \{p-1 : p \text{ prime}\}$ at the outset.** This is exactly what we will need to do.

## 2. BBMST 2022 — improvement to 615,999

**Reference.** Paul Balister, Béla Bollobás, Robert Morris, Julian Sahasrabudhe, Marius Tiba, *On the Erdős covering problem: the density of the uncovered set*, Inventiones 228 (2022), 377–414. arXiv:1811.03547.

### 2.1 What changed

BBMST's chief simplification of Hough: **they eliminate the Lovász Local Lemma entirely.** Instead, they construct probability measures $\mathbb{P}_i$ on $\mathbb{Z}/Q\mathbb{Z}$ directly, parameterized by free real numbers $\delta_1, \ldots, \delta_n \in [0, 1/2]$, and they bound $\mathbb{P}_i(B_i)$ — the probability of the union of progressions exposed at step $i$ — using *only* the 1st and 2nd moment methods (Chebyshev). The "distortion" they introduce is
$$\Delta_i(x) = \max\{0, \log(\mathbb{P}_i(x)/\mathbb{P}_0(x))\},$$
and Lemma 3.5 of their paper gives the average distortion bound
$$\mathbb{E}_i[\Delta_i(x)] \leq 2 \sum_{d \in D_i} \frac{\nu(d)}{d},$$
where $\nu$ is the multiplicative function $\nu(d) = \prod_{p_j \mid d} (1-\delta_j)^{-1}$.

### 2.2 The general theorem (Theorem 3.1)

> **Theorem 3.1 (BBMST 2022).** Let $\mathcal{A} = \{A_d : d \in D\}$ be a finite collection of arithmetic progressions with distinct moduli $d \in D$, and let $\delta_1, \ldots, \delta_n \in [0, 1/2]$. Set
> $$M_i^{(1)} := \mathbb{E}_{i-1}[\alpha_i(x)], \qquad M_i^{(2)} := \mathbb{E}_{i-1}[\alpha_i(x)^2],$$
> where $\alpha_i(x) = |F_{i-1}(x) \cap B_i|/|F_{i-1}(x)|$. If
> $$\eta := \sum_{i=1}^n \min\left\{M_i^{(1)}, \frac{M_i^{(2)}}{4\delta_i(1-\delta_i)}\right\} < 1,$$
> then $\mathcal{A}$ does not cover $\mathbb{Z}$. Moreover, the uncovered set has density at least
> $$(1-\eta) \exp\left(-\frac{2}{1-\eta}\sum_{d \in D} \frac{\nu(d)}{d}\right).$$

Theorem 3.2 bounds the moments:
$$M_i^{(1)} \leq \sum_{m p_i^j \in N_i,\, m \mid Q_{i-1}} p_i^{-j} \cdot \frac{\nu(m)}{m},$$
$$M_i^{(2)} \leq \sum_{m_1 p_i^{j_1}, m_2 p_i^{j_2} \in N_i,\, m_1, m_2 \mid Q_{i-1}} p_i^{-(j_1 + j_2)} \cdot \frac{\nu(\mathrm{lcm}(m_1, m_2))}{\mathrm{lcm}(m_1, m_2)}.$$

### 2.3 The density theorem with weight $\mu$ (Theorem 1.1)

> **Theorem 1.1 (BBMST 2022).** Let $\varepsilon > 0$ and $\mu(p^i) = 1 + (\log p)^{3+\varepsilon}/p$ (extended multiplicatively). There exists $M > 0$ such that if $\mathcal{A}$ has distinct moduli $d_1 \leq \cdots \leq d_k$ with $d_1 \geq M$, and $C = \sum \mu(d_i)/d_i$, then the uncovered set has density at least $e^{-4C}/2$.

This is the "high-leverage tool" for #273: if $\sum_{m \in \mathcal{M}} \mu(m)/m < \infty$, then there is no $\mathcal{M}$-covering. **For $\mathcal{M} = \{p-1\}$, the sum diverges** (Mertens), so direct density-bound rejection is not available. But Theorem 3.1 (the general "does not cover" criterion) does not require $\sum \mu(d)/d < \infty$ — it just needs $\eta < 1$. **This is the entry point for $\mathcal{M}$-specialization.**

### 2.4 Where the 615,999 number comes from

The 615,999 bound (often quoted as 616,000) is BBMST's specialization of Theorem 3.1 to the minimum-modulus problem, with optimized $\delta_i$ values chosen via numerical computation. This is *not* spelled out in the main paper (the bound is just announced in the abstract); the CFT 2022 paper (§4.1) explicitly references this and the BBMST 2022 expository survey (arXiv:2211.01417). The reduction from $10^{16}$ to $\sim 6 \cdot 10^5$ is essentially two-fold:
- Elimination of LLL overhead (∼ factor $10^4$ in the parameter optimization).
- Direct moment-method computation that doesn't need Hough's "good fibre" / "well-distributed" two-step construction.

### 2.5 Other BBMST theorems relevant to #273

- **Theorem 1.2 (Schinzel's conjecture).** Any finite covering system has at least one modulus dividing another. *For #273*: vacuously interesting since some $\mathcal{M}$-pairs divide (e.g., $4 \mid 12$, $6 \mid 12$, $10 \mid 30$).
- **Theorem 1.4 (refined Hough–Nielsen).** Every distinct covering system has $2 \mid Q$, or $9 \mid Q$, or $15 \mid Q$. *For #273*: vacuous since $2 \mid m$ for every $m \in \mathcal{M}$.

## 3. Cummings–Filaseta–Trifonov 2022 — squarefree moduli, bound $\leq 118$

**Reference.** Maria Cummings, Michael Filaseta, Ognian Trifonov, *An upper bound for the minimum modulus in a covering system with squarefree moduli*, arXiv:2211.08548 (Nov 2022).

### 3.1 Theorem statement

> **Theorem 1.1 (CFT 2022).** Every covering system with distinct squarefree moduli has minimum modulus $\leq 118$.

### 3.2 The technical innovation

CFT's contribution is **not** a new methodology — they explicitly write: "Our arguments are not novel in that we basically take the eloquent exposition given by Balister, Bollobás, Morris, Sahasrabudhe and Tiba in [5] and simply refine the arguments to produce our upper bound." What they do, mechanically:

1. **Reformulate the covering question as a hyperplane covering of $Q = S_1 \times \cdots \times S_n$.** Take the distinct primes $p_1 < p_2 < \ldots < p_n$ that divide $\mathrm{lcm}(\mathcal{M})$ and let $S_j = \{1, 2, \ldots, p_j\}$ (squarefree case: every modulus is a product of distinct primes, so its $j$-th coordinate is either a single value in $S_j$ or all of $S_j$).
2. **Use BBMST's Theorem 2.2 in the survey** (their reformulation):
   > Let $\mathcal{A}$ be a collection of hyperplanes that cover $Q = S_1 \times \cdots \times S_n$, no two parallel, and $\liminf_{k \to \infty} |S_k|/k > 3$. Then for any $C > 0$, there exists a hyperplane $A_m \in \mathcal{A}$ with $F(A_m) \subseteq \{1, \ldots, C\}$, where $F(A_m)$ is the set of fixed coordinates.
3. **Tune the $\delta_j$ values explicitly** for $j \in \{1, \ldots, 23, 24, 25, 26, 27, \ldots\}$ with $\delta_j$ ranging from $0$ (for $p_j \leq p_7 = 17$) up to $0.5$ (for $j > 10^6$). The exact sequence:
   - $\delta_1 = \cdots = \delta_7 = 0$ (primes $2, 3, 5, 7, 11, 13, 17$);
   - $\delta_8 = 0.171, \delta_9 = 0.190, \delta_{10} = 0.199, \delta_{11} = 0.210, \delta_{12} = 0.210, \ldots$ (slowly increasing);
   - $\delta_j = 0.331$ for $100 \leq j \leq 1000$, $\delta_j = 0.372$ for $1001 \leq j \leq 10^4$, $\delta_j = 0.418$ for $10^4 \leq j \leq 10^6$, $\delta_j = 0.5$ for $j > 10^6$.
4. **Compute the cumulative bound** $\sum_k w_k(B_k) \leq 0.999385... < 1$, using Maple 2019. The decomposition is:
   - $\sum_{k=1}^7 w_k(B_k) \leq 194/1365 \approx 0.142$ (small primes, hand-computable);
   - $\sum_{k=8}^{10^6} w_k(B_k) \leq 0.857$ (Maple computation);
   - $\sum_{k > 10^6} w_k(B_k) \leq 4 \cdot 10^{-4}$ (asymptotic tail via Lemma 4.7 and Rosser–Schoenfeld effective Mertens).

### 3.3 Where squarefreeness enters

Three load-bearing places:

1. **$|S_j| = p_j$ exactly** for every prime. This is because a squarefree modulus $m$ either includes the prime $p_j$ exactly once (in which case it fixes one of the $p_j$ values in coordinate $j$) or excludes $p_j$ entirely (in which case it doesn't constrain coordinate $j$ at all). If moduli could be divisible by $p_j^2$, then $|S_j|$ would have to be $p_j^2$ to account for the additional residue precision, and the bias-statistic estimates explode.
2. **Lemma 4.7's Mertens-style estimate** $\sum_{p \leq x} 1/p \leq \log\log x + B + 1/(2\log^2 x)$ applied to $\sum_{j=N+1}^{k-1} 1/|S_j| = \sum_{p_{N+1} \leq p \leq p_{k-1}} 1/p$. This identification $|S_j| = p_j$ is what lets them invoke effective Mertens directly.
3. **The hyperplane $\nu(F(A))$ count.** For a squarefree modulus $m = \prod_{j \in F} p_j$, the weight $\nu(F(A)) = \prod_{j \in F} (1-\delta_j)^{-1}/p_j$. This product structure lets them bound $w_k(A) \leq \nu(F(A))$ inductively. Non-squarefree moduli would have terms like $1/(p^2)$ for $p^2 \| m$ that complicate the multiplicative structure.

### 3.4 Could similar restriction-techniques apply to $\mathcal{M}$?

**Yes, with caveats.** The relevant generalization:
- $\mathcal{M}$ is *not* squarefree (e.g., $4 = 2^2, 16 = 2^4, 12 = 2^2 \cdot 3, 36 = 2^2 \cdot 3^2$ are in $\mathcal{M}$), so the squarefree-specific simplification doesn't apply.
- However, $\mathcal{M}$ has a specific *prime-multiplicity distribution*: $\nu_p(m)$ for $m = q-1$ has known distribution (e.g., $\nu_2(p-1)$ has known density by Chinese-restricted classes).
- The right formal generalization: instead of $S_j = \{1, \ldots, p_j\}$ (squarefree) or $S_j = \{1, \ldots, p_j^{\gamma_j}\}$ (general moduli of bounded $p_j$-valuation), one should take $S_j = \{1, \ldots, p_j^{\gamma_j}\}$ with $\gamma_j = \max_{m \in \mathcal{M}} \nu_{p_j}(m)$ — but restrict the hyperplanes that *appear* in $\mathcal{A}$ to only those that correspond to moduli of the form $p-1$.

The key auxiliary estimate this requires: **for fixed prime $p_j$ and integer $\gamma$, the density of primes $q$ such that $\nu_{p_j}(q-1) = \gamma$.** This is a classical Galois-theoretic / Chebotarev question with known answers:
- $\nu_{p_j}(q-1) \geq \gamma \iff q \equiv 1 \pmod{p_j^\gamma}$ (Fermat); by Dirichlet, density $1/\varphi(p_j^\gamma) = 1/p_j^{\gamma-1}(p_j-1)$ among primes.
- $\nu_{p_j}(q-1) = \gamma \iff q \equiv 1 \pmod{p_j^\gamma}, q \not\equiv 1 \pmod{p_j^{\gamma+1}}$; density $(1 - 1/p_j)/p_j^{\gamma-1}(p_j-1) = (p_j - 1)^{-1} p_j^{-\gamma + 1}$ minus refinements.

In particular, the *number* of $\mathcal{M}$-elements of the form $\{q - 1 : q \text{ prime}, q \leq X, \nu_{p_j}(q-1) = \gamma\}$ is approximately
$$\frac{1}{(p_j - 1) p_j^{\gamma-1}} \cdot \frac{X}{\log X} \quad \text{(by Siegel-Walfisz)}.$$

**This is the precise input needed to plug $\mathcal{M}$ into BBMST Theorem 3.1.**

## 4. Klein–Koukoulopoulos–Lemieux 2022 — $j$-th smallest modulus bound

**Reference.** Jonah Klein, Dimitris Koukoulopoulos, Simon Lemieux, *On the $j$-th smallest modulus of a covering system with distinct moduli*, arXiv:2212.01299 (Dec 2022, rev. Aug 2023).

### 4.1 Theorem statements

> **Theorem 1 (KKL 2022).** There exists an absolute constant $c > 0$ such that the $j$-th smallest modulus in a minimal covering system with distinct moduli is $\leq \exp(cj^2/\log(j+1))$.

> **Theorem 3 (KKL 2022).** Let $\mathcal{A}$ be a covering system of multiplicity $s$. Then there exists an absolute constant $c > 0$ such that its smallest modulus is $\leq \exp(c\log^2(s+1)/\log\log(s+2))$.

Theorem 2 gives a *construction* (lower bound): for each $j \geq 5$ there exists a minimal covering system with $j$ moduli of size $2 < 2^2 < 2^3 < \ldots < 2^{j-4} < 3 \cdot 2^{j-5} < 2^{j-3} < 3 \cdot 2^{j-4} < 3 \cdot 2^{j-3}$, exhibiting that the bound is tight up to logarithmic factors in the exponent.

### 4.2 Method

KKL uses BBMST's distortion machinery (specifically BBMST's Theorem 3.1) plus Crittenden–Vanden Eynden's "if $2^\ell$ consecutive integers are covered, then $\mathbb{Z}$ is covered" theorem. The argument: if a minimal covering system has $k$ moduli, the multiplicity bound (BBMST) gives a bound on the smallest modulus; combining with the CVE-style argument turns this into a bound on the $j$-th smallest modulus.

### 4.3 Is $c$ explicit?

**No.** Theorem 3 in KKL's proof concludes "If $c$ is large enough compared to $C$ (which we have already fixed), then ... ." The constant $c$ depends on the choice of moment-bound constant $C$, which in turn depends on the BBMST 3.1 parameters. Tracing through the chain, $c$ is *in principle* explicit but is not given numerically in the paper. **An explicit $c$ would require redoing the BBMST 615,999 optimization plus an additional convexity argument — likely 1 session of focused analyst work.**

### 4.4 Relevance to $\mathcal{M}$

KKL's bound applies to *any* distinct-moduli covering. If a covering by $\mathcal{M}$ exists with $k$ moduli, then its $j$-th smallest modulus is $\leq \exp(cj^2/\log(j+1))$. **This gives a per-element upper bound, so for any fixed $j$ the search space for the $j$-th smallest is finite.** Combined with the Klein–Koukoulopoulos–Lemieux 2024 sum-of-reciprocals bound, this is a *useful* lever for the constructive SAT-search vector (V2 in our project) — it tells us how far up the $j$-th element of an $\mathcal{M}$-covering can be. But it does not by itself rule out $\mathcal{M}$-coverings.

## 5. Specialization to $\mathcal{M}$ — what would be needed

Concrete sub-lemmas, with technical content estimates. **Order: simplest first.**

### 5.1 ($\mathcal{M}$-density estimate, 1 session, analyst)

**Lemma:** For each prime $p_j \geq 2$ and each integer $\gamma \geq 1$, the number of $\mathcal{M}$-elements of size $\leq X$ with $\nu_{p_j}(m) = \gamma$ is
$$|\{m \in \mathcal{M} : m \leq X, \nu_{p_j}(m) = \gamma\}| = \frac{1}{(p_j-1) p_j^{\gamma-1}} \cdot \frac{X}{\log X} \cdot (1 + o(1))$$
by Siegel–Walfisz applied to primes in arithmetic progression $q \equiv 1 \pmod{p_j^\gamma}$, $q \not\equiv 1 \pmod{p_j^{\gamma+1}}$.

**Use:** This is the explicit count needed to instantiate BBMST Theorem 3.2 (moment bounds) for $\mathcal{M}$-restricted moduli sums. The slot it goes into:
$$M_i^{(1)} \leq \sum_{m p_i^j \in N_i \cap \mathcal{M}_{\text{rel}},\, m \mid Q_{i-1}} p_i^{-j} \cdot \frac{\nu(m)}{m},$$
where $\mathcal{M}_{\text{rel}}$ is the $\mathcal{M}$-restricted version of the new-moduli set.

**Complexity:** Standard analytic number theory. Mathlib has Dirichlet's theorem on primes in APs (`Mathlib.NumberTheory.DirichletCharacter`); Siegel-Walfisz is *not* in Mathlib but the qualitative version (Dirichlet density) is. Effective version requires Page–Siegel zero analysis, which is harder to formalize.

### 5.2 (Hyperplane reformulation for $\mathcal{M}$, 1-2 sessions, analyst + formalist)

**Lemma:** A covering system with all moduli in $\mathcal{M}$ corresponds to a *restricted* hyperplane covering of $Q = S_1 \times S_2 \times \cdots$, where each $S_j = \{1, \ldots, p_j^{\gamma_j}\}$ (with $\gamma_j = \max_{m \in \mathcal{M}_{Q_n}} \nu_{p_j}(m)$, a finite quantity per stage), but the *available* hyperplanes are only those whose fixed-coordinate set $F(A)$ corresponds to a number of the form $\prod p_j^{\gamma_j(A)} = q - 1$ for some prime $q \geq 5$.

**Use:** This is the cleanest formal restatement of #273 in BBMST's hyperplane language. The cardinality $|\mathcal{A}_\mathcal{M}|$ of available hyperplanes is *much smaller* than the full $|\mathcal{A}|$ admissible in the general distinct-moduli problem — by 5.1, only $\sim X/\log X$ of the $X$ candidate moduli are admissible. This *should* drive a tighter $\eta < 1$ bound in BBMST Theorem 3.1.

**Complexity:** Roughly 30–50 lines of Lean, mostly definitions and a bijection lemma.

### 5.3 ($\mathcal{M}$-specific moment bound, 2-3 sessions, analyst)

**Lemma:** Under the $\mathcal{M}$-restriction, the moments satisfy
$$M_i^{(1)} \ll \sum_{m p_i^j \in N_i,\, m p_i^j + 1 \text{ prime}} p_i^{-j} \cdot \frac{\nu(m)}{m},$$
which by 5.1 is bounded by
$$M_i^{(1)} \ll \frac{1}{\log(p_i \cdot \mathrm{lcm}(N_i))} \sum_{m p_i^j \in N_i} p_i^{-j} \cdot \frac{\nu(m)}{m}.$$
The extra $1/\log$ factor is the "savings" from the $\mathcal{M}$-density.

**Use:** Plug into BBMST Theorem 3.1 to get an $\eta_\mathcal{M} < \eta_{\text{full}}$. Concretely, this should let us replace 615,999 (the BBMST general bound) with something sharper for $\mathcal{M}$-coverings.

**Complexity:** Genuine new analytic argument. The $1/\log X$ savings should propagate through the moment-iteration into a smaller minimum-modulus bound. This is the *novel content* of the V3 vector.

### 5.4 (Effective constants under $\mathcal{M}$, 1 session, analyst)

**Lemma:** The "615,999" of BBMST, when redone with the $\mathcal{M}$-restricted moment bounds 5.3, yields a sharper explicit constant $C_\mathcal{M}$. We expect $C_\mathcal{M} \in [10^3, 10^5]$ heuristically; the explicit number depends on optimization over $\delta_j$ parameters (à la CFT's Maple computation).

**Complexity:** This is the *numerical optimization* step — analogous to CFT's Maple computation. Would need to be implemented in Python via `sympy` or similar. Note: CFT 2022 took the squarefree restriction and got $118$. The $\mathcal{M}$-restriction is *weaker* than squarefree (we can have $4 = 2^2, 16 = 2^4$ in $\mathcal{M}$ but those aren't squarefree); but the $\mathcal{M}$-restriction *adds* a density gain ($\sim 1/\log$ per prime layer). Quantitative comparison is unclear without the computation; this is precisely what V3 should resolve.

### 5.5 ($\mathcal{M}$-densitiy theorem analog, 3-5 sessions, analyst + critic)

**Lemma:** If $\mathcal{A}$ is a finite covering by $\mathcal{M}$-moduli with minimum modulus $\geq M_\mathcal{M}$, where $M_\mathcal{M}$ is the bound from 5.4, then either (a) no such covering exists (the $\eta_\mathcal{M} < 1$ constraint is violated), or (b) the uncovered set has positive density $\geq f(\sum \mu(m)/m \cdot [\text{Mertens-density factor}])$.

This is the full $\mathcal{M}$-analog of BBMST Theorem 1.1.

**Complexity:** Probably feasible in 3-5 sessions with the analyst agent. Higher leverage than 5.4 because it gives a *density bound* rather than just a min-modulus bound, putting us into the BBMST 1.1 framework directly.
